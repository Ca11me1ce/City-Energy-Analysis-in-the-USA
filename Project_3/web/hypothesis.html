Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is an act in statistics whereby an analyst tests an assumption regarding a population parameter. The methodology employed by the analyst depends on the nature of the data used and the reason for the analysis. Hypothesis testing is used to infer the result of a hypothesis performed on sample data from a larger population.

For the residential dataset and T-test for this case, a hypothesis – the difference between the means of ‘elec_degree’ and ‘gas_degree’ attributes is not obvious. The statistics is 31.557228736693705 and the p-value is 2.5842736854136347e-216. It is false to satisfy the conditional statement p-value > 0.05. The original hypothesis fails. Therefore, the difference between the degrees of electricity and natural gas is obvious. It represents the distribution of electricity degrees is different from the distribution of natural gas degrees.

For the commercial dataset and T-test for this case, the result is as following:

Hypothesis: The mean difference between attribute elec_score and attribute gas_score are not significant.

The p-value in LeveneResult of these two attributes is larger than 0.05, which means these two attributes have homogeneity of variance. Set equal_val to True and get the p-value in Ttest_indResult is also larger than 0.05, which means hypothesis 1 is true: The mean difference between attribute elec_score and attribute gas_score are not significant.

ANOVA for this case, the result is as following:

Hypothesis: Attribute elec_1kdollars has significant influence on attribute elec_class.

df represents freedom; Sum_sq represents sum of squares; Mean_sq means mean the sum of square; F represents the value of F test statistic; PR(>F) represents the test p-value. The p-value of this hypothesis is 0.00000, which means that the result is highly significant. Therefore, hypothesis 2 is true: Attribute elec_1kdollars has a significant influence on attribute elec_class.

For the industrial dataset and T-test for these cases, There is the first hypothesis - the difference between the mean of elec_bin_group and gas_bin_group attributes are not obvious. Ttest_indResult(statistic=-0.05563937437162836, pvalue=0.955629731523744). The p-value is greater than 0.05? True. From the result, that shows the first hypothesis is supported. Therefore, the difference between the mean of elec_bin_group and gas_bin_group attributes is not obvious. The T-test is good for the paired samples to test in this case.

There is the second hypothesis - the difference between the mean of elec_score and gas_score attributes is not obvious. Ttest_indResult(statistic=-16.468126914649144, pvalue=1.7708281169593017e-60). The p-value is greater than 0.05? False. From the result, that shows the second hypothesis is failed. Therefore, the difference between the mean of elec_score and gas_score attributes is obvious. The T-test is good for the paired samples to test in this case.

For residential dataset and linear regression for this case, another hypothesis – the ‘housing_units’ and ‘total_pop’- is positively related with ‘elec_degree’. The fit coefficient is 0.7770601547487651. The hypothesis is true.

For the industrial dataset and logistic regression for this case, Hypothesis: Attribute elec_score, num_establishment, elec_1kdollars, elec_1b_ghg have a significant influence on attribute elec_class.

Look at the column P > |z|, this column represents the p-value. Since all attribute’s p-value is smaller than 0.001, the attribute elec_score, num_establishment, elec_1kdollars, elec_1b_ghg have significant influence on attribute elec_class. Therefore, the hypothesis is true.
